4 2016-01-25 Monday

We're going to talk about the brain and the things that go on in it.

  • Cerebral cortex - outer portion of the brain, roughly 80%. Contains millions of nerves (neurons)
  • Neuron - basic cell that specializes in electrically transmitting information through the brain
    • Cell body - makes new components, etc.
    • Dendrite - tendrils coming off the cell body, they receive information from other cells.
    • Axon - a longer branch from the cell body, which transmits information to other cells.
    • Myelin Sheath - helps speed up/insulate the transmission of impulses
    • At the end of the axon (the terminal buttons), you find Synapses
    • When a signal "fires" through the axon, some chemicals are released which cross the synapse and start the impulse in the next neuron
  • Neural Communication
    • Within the neuron, transmission is electrical
    • Between the neurons, transmission is chemical
  • Neurotransmitters
    • Serotonin - regulates mood and depression, appetite, sleep, sensory stuff
      • People who suffer from depressions frequently report disruptions in appetite and sleep
    • Acetylcholine - involved in voluntary movement, learning, sleep
      • Too much can be associated with depression
      • Too little in the hippocampus (?) can be associated with dimensia
    • Dopamine - makes you feel "good" - part of the "reward system". Also involved with motor control.
    • Endorphins - part of pain management system
      • painkillers are similar in structure to endorphins
  • Three types of neurons:
    • Sensory neurons
    • Motor neurons
      • mirror: "feel" what other people are feeling, empathy, etc.
    • Interneurons
  • Nervous System
    • Central Nervous System (CNS) = nerves within the brain and spinal cord
    • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) = nerves outside brain and spinal cord
      • Somatic or Voluntary (skeletal muscles)
      • Autonomic (smooth and cardiac muscles and glands)
        • Parasympathetic branch (homeostasis, daily maintenance)
        • Sympathetic branch (alert system)
  • Major areas of the brain
    • forebrain
      • frontal lobe - planning, organizing, "executive functions", emotions, social judgements, etc
      • temporal lobe -
      • parietal lobe - sensory & perceptual info,
      • occipital lobe -
    • midbrain
      • helps respond to visual/audio stimuli with muscle movements
      • some memory
    • hindbrain - most primitive, but essential part of the brain
      • regulates air, blood, consciousness, etc.
  • We have those "lobes" on both sides of the brain, but we also have two "hemispheres"
    • each side is responsible for the opposite side's motion.
    • Left Hemisphere: language & speech, motor functions of the right hand & leg, sensory functions of the right hand & leg;
      • infants will do more thumb sucking when language is heard in their right ear (which is sent to the left hemisphere)
    • Right Hemisphere: spatial & non-verbal, (plus motor/sensory functions of left side).
      • Aprosodia - condition where you are unable to detect or produce intonation and tones in people's voices.
        • damaged right frontal lobe = you can't "produce" variation
        • damaged right temporal lobe = you can't interpret/receive this variation.
    • Corpus callosum - a band of fibers through which information can be transferred between hemispheres

Three Principles of Sensation & Perception

  • Adaptive processes
    • once you see something a certain way (eg. the cow picture), it's hard to see it any other way
  • Discrepancy between physical & psychological
  • Active processes
    • changes as you look

Gestalt Principles of Form Perception

  • Similarity - We group things by their similarity to each other
  • Proximity - we group things by their proximity
  • Good continuation - we combine things into larger items
  • Simplicity
  • Common Fate - as things move, if they move together, we perceive them as a single unit.
  • Closure - we tend to interpret things with depth and "close" shapes

Perceptual Constancy

  • Color - tendency to perceive an object's color as stable even though they're changing. EG the colors due to light and shadow are different, but we perceive them as th same because we compensate for the shadow.
  • Shape - the tendency for an object's shape to appear consistent and constant. The examples here were all objects in different 3D orientations, but we perceive them as all the same shape.
  • Size - again, we compensate for depth in images, and we make adjustments for size.

Quiz 2 was today.

Music (especially in movies) has emotional effects. We can learn to associate what happens in music with what happens on screen. Then next time we hear the music we physically/emotionally respond.

This process is classical conditioning.

  • Unconditioned Stimulus - stimulus that we don't learn (eg person getting ripped apart)
  • Unconditioned Response - response to stimulus that we don't need to learn.
  • Neutral stimulus - something we don't have a learned/unconditioned response for.
  • conditioned stimulus - something we have learned a response for.
  • conditioned response - response we have learned.

Pavlov's Dog!

  • meat: UCS
  • bell: neutral stimulus
  • after conditioning, the bell was a conditioned stimulus

Sensory memory.

Iconic storage

  • visual
  • duration of .5 - 2 seconds.
  • flip-o-rama

Echoic storage

  • auditory
  • duration of 3-4 seconds

Information Processing Model

  • Stimuli first go to sensory registers. If we do nothing with it, the information is lost.
  • If we decide to do something with it, it goes into our short-term memory (STM).
    • From STM, if you continue to rehearse it, you can maintain it in short term memory. maintenance rehearsal
  • Long Term Memory (LTM)
    • You can retrieve items from LTM into STM

Short Term Memory (STM)

  • Holds the information to which we are consciously attending.
  • Short term memory task

XNBCPHDSATCBSX

Chunking

  • group information into large chunks. For example, in the above string of letters:
  • X NBC PHD SAT CBS X
  • Only 5 pieces of information
  • Facilitates moving into LTM by relating to information you already know.

Short-term memory and long-term memory are not the same as recent and remote.

Organizing information in Long-Term Memory

  • Parallel Distributed Processing (PDP) Model
    • Represent information/details as nodes in a graph.
  • Spreading activation theory.

Types of long-term memory

  • Explicit (conscious)
    • Processed in hippocampus
    • Facts - general knowledge (semantic)
    • Personal experience (episodic)
    • "Declarative"
  • Implicit (unconscious)
    • Processed in cerebellum
    • Skills - motor and cognitive.
    • Condition effects.
    • Procedural.

Loss of info from LTM

  • Decay theory - "it just fades"
  • Interference theory - "something is blocking you from being able to recall."
    • "It's on the tip of my tongue!"
  • Motivated forgetting - "we'll get to this later"

Intelligence

It's complicated to define intelligence. Theorists have apparently argued about it for a long time. Over time, people have recognized that it is multi-faceted. And apparently these are some of the facets:

  • Knowledge: we tend to think that people who know a lot are smart. We recognize that how much you know isn't the only part of intelligence, but frequently knowledge corresponds with interest and intellectual discipline that we respect as parts of Intelligence as well.
  • Culture: Different cultures respect different mental capabilities.
  • Problem Solving
  • Social Intuitions & Skills: good at communicating with people in a way that can get them to agree with their arguments.

Psychometric Approach

  • Statistical techniques → intellectual skills and abilities
  • IQ = (Mental Age / Chronological Age) × 100
  • People execute tasks and get ranked on their mental age (based on statistics of other people's performances). This is used to get their IQ
  • Wechsler Intelligence Scales: Full Scale IQ → Verbal IQ, Performance IQ
  • So, lots of statistics on people's performance on tests.

Understanding Intelligence

  • Psychometric Approach
  • Information-Processing Approach
  • Multiple Intelligences

Information Processing Approach

  • Knowledge Base
  • Speed of Processing
  • Acquisition & Application

Multiple Intelligences Approach

  • "Intelligence is a function of multiple systems."
    • Kind of like how there are different types of IQ scores that contribute to the full-scale IQ.
  • The idea is that these different "intelligences" or skills frequently go together; that is, somebody who has high verbal skills will tend to score higher in other areas as well.
  • However, the MI approach also seems to suggest (unlike Psychometric Approach) that these intelligence areas also stand on their own, and aren't necessarily part of a larger general intelligence score.

Personality

  • Personality is what predicts your "default" behavior.
  • Things people observe (your behavior, how you treat people, etc) are observations that can allow someone to predict your personality.

Personality Types

  • A/B: Type A = heavily organized and structured, Type B = not so heavily organized or structured.
  • Introvert/Extrovert: Introvert = somebody who "finds their energy" by being alone, extrovert = somebody who "recharges" by being around people.
  • Astrology (predictions about your personality based on your birth month/year)
  • Buzzfeed "personality quizzes". These are symptomatic of how our culture views personality, as something you can observe and then assign labels to.
  • Myers-Briggs personality types.

Critiques of Typologies

  • Not reliable - frequently they'll give you different answers, or they'll change or time.
    • Especially if personality is supposed to be stable.
  • False dichotomies don't allow for variation (e.g. introverts may sometimes be energized by interaction with others)

Personality Flexibility is Important

  • Intra-Personally
    • Introverts need to be able to interact, extroverts need to be able to spend time alone
    • Inflexible personality is a hallmark of personality disorder
  • Inter-Personally
    • Societies need different kinds of people.

Trait Models

  • Allow for variation along a spectrum of traits.
  • These spectra frequently look something like a normal distribution. Most people are "boring", see 68-95-99.7.
    • However, although not mentioned in this lecture, I would also say that even though most people are in the average, the Law of Averages says that people are rarely "completely average". It's pretty darn unlikely to be average in every single trait, so it's pretty normal for people to have a couple defining characteristics, even though overall, they're "normal" or average.

Where do Trait Models Come From

  • Lexical hypothesis: all salient and socially relevant personality differences appear in language.
  • Factor analysis: groups highly correlated items.
    • 16 primary traits according to Cattell
    • 5 global traits according to most everyone else.
  • These are just "trait" adjectives.

Big Five

  • Openness to experience: (inventive/curious vs consistent/cautious)
    • High Openness:
      • Criteria: Wide interests, original, curious, artistic, imaginitive
    • Low Openness:
      • Criteria: Conventional, simple
  • Conscientiousness
  • Extroversion/Introversion
    • High extroversion:
      • Pros: broad social network, abundant energy, protection vs depression.
      • Cons: conspicuousness, heavy attention needs, etc.
  • Agreeableness
  • Neuroticism vs Emotional Stability
    • High Neuroticism:
      • Criteria: depressed, moody, tense, worrisome
      • Benefits: caution, reactivity to faint danger signals, sensitivity to others' emotions
      • Costs: stress, anxiety, etc
    • Low Neuroticism:
      • Criteria: emotionally stable, calm

Big 5 Model Mnemonic: OCEAN
